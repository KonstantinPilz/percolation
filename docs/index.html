Phase transitions

How gradual change builds, invisibly, until it tips into a sudden step change — shown through percolation, the cleanest model of the idea.

Push a smooth control — temperature, density, the number of connections — and for a long while nothing much happens. Then, within a hair of a critical value, the system flips: scattered parts lock into one whole, all at once. That is a phase transition, and it is the native shape of "slow, slow, slow, then sudden."

Every model below shares one skeleton, the order parameter: drag the input axis left to right and watch the output stay flat, flat, flat — then break almost vertically at the threshold. The same threshold math describes economic networks linking up and security cascades propagating, which is why one picture can carry both.

Each figure is a real simulation running live in your browser, not a drawing. Drag the sliders.

01

The square lattice

Fill cells of a grid at random with probability p. Below p ≈ 0.593 you get scattered islands; cross it and a single cluster suddenly spans top to bottom. The canonical phase transition.

02

Hexagons, and an exact one-half

A hex grid is percolation on the triangular lattice — and its threshold is provably exactly p = ½, no decimals. A cleaner number, and a more deliberate-looking tiling.

03

Off the grid: growing disks

No lattice at all — just disks scattered in the plane, grown until they overlap. Near 68% coverage the isolated blobs merge into one spanning region. The most literal "shapes growing until they suddenly touch."

04

Explosive percolation: the score

The same transition drawn two ways. Random links grow a giant cluster as a gentle ramp; links chosen to suppress big merges hold it flat, then jump almost vertically. How you add the connections sets how sharp the step is.

05

Explosive percolation: the networks

The same two rules, now as the actual graphs. Same 180 nodes, same number of links — the left fills in gradually while the right stays shattered, then snaps whole. The gap between the panels is the explosive transition.

06

When the medium is a fractal

Mandelbrot percolation: the square is recursively subdivided and cells kept at random, forever, so the surviving set is a true fractal. Lower the keep-probability and it doesn't fade — it abruptly shatters into disconnected dust. A picture of fragility.

07

A tiling that never repeats

Percolation on a Penrose tiling — perfect five-fold symmetry, yet no pattern ever repeats. The transition is still clean and universal: at the threshold, the particular tiling stops mattering.

Models implemented from scratch (union-find connectivity, golden-ratio deflation for the Penrose tiling, the Achlioptas product rule for explosive percolation). Curves and tilings regenerate on each load, so exact shapes vary.
